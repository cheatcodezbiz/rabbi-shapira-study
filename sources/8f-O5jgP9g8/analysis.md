## 1. Title

**尼散月：末世預言、波斯衝突與彌賽亞的顯現**
**Nisan: End-Times Prophecy, Persian Conflict, and the Messiah's Revelation**

## 2. One-Sentence Thesis

Rabbi Shapira claims that current geopolitical events involving Iran and Arab nations, particularly around the Strait of Hormuz, are direct fulfillments of ancient Jewish prophecies (from Rabbi Laniado, *Yalkut Shimoni*, and *Genesis Rabbah*) indicating the imminent arrival of the Messiah (specifically Messiah ben Joseph) and the final redemption, all accelerating during the month of Nisan.

## 3. Argument Map

1.  **Urgency of the Message:** Rabbi Shapira delivers an urgent, "shocking, chilling prophecy" just before Rosh Chodesh Nisan, emphasizing the need for readiness and prayer.
2.  **Rabbi Laniado's 500-Year-Old Prophecy:** He introduces a prophecy by Rabbi Laniado, based on Isaiah 34:6, which speaks of a "great slaughter" (*Tevach Gadol*) in Betzara/Botsra, identified as the War of Gog and Magog.
3.  **Identification of Betzara/Botsra:** Laniado's prophecy identifies Betzara as a land "far from Babylon," on the border of Assyria and Iran, between Edom and the Strait of Hormuz, controlled by Ishmael (Iran).
4.  **Hormuz as the Starting Point:** The prophecy suggests the War of Gog and Magog "perhaps" begins in the Strait of Hormuz, which is currently a center of conflict.
5.  **Iran's Role (Ishmael):** This war will be led, supervised, or controlled by Ishmael (Iran) during the *Acharit Hayamim* (End of Days).
6.  **Nisan as the Month of Redemption:** Rabbi Shapira asserts that Nisan is the "month of redemption" (*Geula*) and an "accelerated phase" in the redemptive process, expecting miraculous events between now and Passover.
7.  **Yalkut Shimoni Remez 499 and Isaiah 60:1:** He introduces *Yalkut Shimoni Remez 499*, which interprets Isaiah 60:1 ("Kumi Ori" - Arise, shine) by stating that "In the year the King Messiah is to be revealed, the kings of the nations of the world will mock each other."
8.  **Current Events as Prophetic Fulfillment:** Rabbi Shapira connects this Midrash directly to current events, where Iran is "mocking other Arab countries" (Saudi Arabia, UAE, Bahrain) and launching military strikes against them, fulfilling the prophecy of "Persian King mocking Arab King" and "Arabs attacking Arabs."
9.  **Messianic Birth Pangs (*Chevlei Mashiach*):** The Midrash continues, describing the King of Arabia seeking advice and the King of Persia returning to "utterly destroy the whole world," leading to "endless pain, like the unbearable, intense pain of a woman in childbirth," which Rabbi Shapira identifies as the beginning of the Messianic birth pangs.
10. **Temple Mount Movement:** He predicts "movement concerning the Temple" in the coming days, weeks, or months, suggesting changes on the Temple Mount (e.g., Israel taking it, an earthquake leveling it).
11. **Nisan as the Prophetic Timing:** He highlights that Rabbis rarely speak of specific times, but *Yalkut Shimoni Remez 499* is an exception, stating that "The patriarchs of the world are destined to tremble in Nisan," indicating that redemption will begin in Nisan during a war with Persia.
12. **Messiah ben Ephraim (Messiah ben Joseph):** The Midrash describes the patriarchs speaking to *Mashach ben Ephraim* (Messiah ben Joseph), who is depicted as a suffering Messiah bearing the sins and harshness for Israel, mocked by nations. Rabbi Shapira explicitly identifies this suffering Messiah with Yeshua, noting this is the image of Ben Joseph *before* Ben David.
13. **Dialogue in Nisan:** The crucial point is that this dialogue between the patriarchs and Messiah ben Ephraim occurs "in Nisan," reinforcing the current month's significance.
14. **Messiah's Release During Persian War:** The Midrash further states that the Holy One will lift the Messiah to the heavens, and God will tell Messiah ben Ephraim to "judge these people" (the wicked Persians). This "release" of Ephraim into the world will happen "during a war with Persia, which is Iran."
15. **The Concept of "First" (*Rishon*):** Rabbi Shapira draws on *Genesis Rabbah* to connect Nisan (the first month) with other "firsts": God, Torah, festivals, Temple, Israel, and even Persia (as "leader" or "first enemy"). This "collision happens among the 'First.'"
16. **Messiah as "First":** *Genesis Rabbah* also calls the King Messiah "the first," linking him to Esau (the firstborn who was ruddy) and the first redemption.
17. **Purim and Nisan's Significance:** He references Esther 3:7, where Haman cast the *Pur* (lot, Persian for "fate") on Rosh Chodesh Nisan to determine Israel's destruction. He applies the principle of *Middah k'neged middah* (measure for measure), believing this lot will now fall on Iran in Nisan.
18. **Redemption Begins and Ends in Nisan:** Citing the Rosh Hashanah tractate, he concludes that "Redemption will first begin in Nisan, and will also end in Nisan."
19. **Call to Action:** He urges listeners to celebrate Passover, believe in the great redemption, expect supernatural miracles in Nisan, and for Israel to win its "battle of faith," praying for redemption to come soon and for Amalek to be destroyed.

## 5. Key Hebrew Terms

*   **Tevach Gadol (טבח גדול)** – "Great slaughter."
    *   **Gloss:** A massive killing or massacre.
    *   **Why it matters:** Rabbi Shapira, citing Rabbi Laniado, interprets this term from Isaiah 34:6 as referring specifically to the War of Gog and Magog, a pivotal end-times conflict.
*   **Acharit Hayamim (אחרית הימים)** – "End of Days" or "Latter Days."
    *   **Gloss:** The eschatological period leading up to and including the Messianic era.
    *   **Why it matters:** Rabbi Shapira uses this term to frame the current geopolitical events and prophecies as unfolding within the final period of history before the Messiah's arrival.
*   **Geula (גאולה)** – "Redemption."
    *   **Gloss:** The ultimate salvation and liberation of Israel and the world, often associated with the coming of the Messiah.
    *   **Why it matters:** Rabbi Shapira emphasizes that Nisan is the "month of redemption" and that the current events are an "accelerated phase" of this ongoing process.
*   **Kumi Ori (קומי אורי)** – "Arise, shine."
    *   **Gloss:** A command from Isaiah 60:1, calling Israel to rise up and reflect God's light.
    *   **Why it matters:** This verse is the starting point for the *Yalkut Shimoni* prophecy that Rabbi Shapira connects to the current mocking of kings and the onset of Messianic events.
*   **Chevlei Mashiach (חבלי משיח)** – "Messianic birth pangs."
    *   **Gloss:** The period of intense suffering, distress, and upheaval that is prophesied to precede the coming of the Messiah, likened to labor pains.
    *   **Why it matters:** Rabbi Shapira identifies the current global turmoil, particularly the conflict involving Iran and Arab nations, as the beginning of these birth pangs, signaling the Messiah's imminent arrival.
*   **Mashach ben Ephraim (משיח בן אפרים) / Mashach ben Yosef (משיח בן יוסף)** – "Messiah son of Ephraim" / "Messiah son of Joseph."
    *   **Gloss:** The first of two traditional Messianic figures in Jewish thought, often depicted as a suffering warrior Messiah who prepares the way for Messiah ben David and may die in battle.
    *   **Why it matters:** Rabbi Shapira explicitly identifies the suffering Messiah described in *Yalkut Shimoni* with Yeshua, presenting this as the image of Ben Joseph *before* Ben David, and states that the patriarchs are "awakening" this Messiah.
*   **Rishon (ראשון)** – "First."
    *   **Gloss:** The primary, initial, or foremost.
    *   **Why it matters:** Rabbi Shapira, citing *Genesis Rabbah*, highlights the recurring theme of "firstness" connected to God, Torah, festivals, Temple, Israel, Persia, and the Messiah, emphasizing that Nisan (the first month) is the time when these "firsts" collide and redemption unfolds.
*   **Pur (פור)** – "Lot" or "Fate."
    *   **Gloss:** A Persian word for "lot," as in casting lots.
    *   **Why it matters:** Rabbi Shapira connects Haman's casting of the lot in Nisan (Esther 3:7) to the current situation, suggesting that the "lot from heaven" is now falling on Iran, signifying their impending downfall.
*   **Middah k'neged middah (מידה כנגד מידה)** – "Measure for measure."
    *   **Gloss:** The principle of divine justice where punishment or reward corresponds to the nature of the action.
    *   **Why it matters:** Rabbi Shapira applies this principle to Haman's lot, suggesting that just as Haman sought to destroy Israel in Nisan, now the lot of destruction will fall upon Iran in Nisan.

## 6. Key Gematria / Numerical Claims

Rabbi Shapira does not present explicit gematria calculations (numerical values of Hebrew letters). However, he makes a significant conceptual claim based on the idea of "firstness" (*Rishon*):

*   **The "First" (Rishon) Connection:** He repeatedly emphasizes that Nisan is the "first month" and connects this "firstness" to various entities and concepts:
    *   God is called "First."
    *   His word, Torah, is called "First."
    *   The festivals are called "First."
    *   The chosen Temple is called "First."
    *   Israel, His people, are called "First."
    *   Their enemy, Persia, was called "Leader" (first).
    *   The King Messiah is called "First."
    *   The first redemption happened in the first month (Nisan/Passover), and the future redemption will also happen in the first month.
    *   **Why it matters:** This conceptual linking of "firstness" across divine, sacred, national, adversarial, and Messianic realms suggests a divinely ordained pattern and timing, indicating that the current events in Nisan are part of a pre-ordained "collision among the 'First'" that will usher in the final redemption.

## 9. A Word on Discernment

It is crucial for the article author and readers to understand that Rabbi Shapira's teaching, while deeply rooted in Jewish tradition, primarily employs a method of interpretation known as *drash* (homiletical or interpretive exposition) rather than *peshat* (the plain, literal meaning). This is a legitimate and rich form of Jewish hermeneutics, but it means that certain claims are interpretive connections rather than direct, literal predictions.

Specifically:

*   **Identification of Betzara/Botsra with Hormuz/Iran:** While Isaiah 34:6 mentions Betzara, its precise geographical location is debated. Rabbi Laniado's interpretation, identifying it with the border of Assyria and Iran, the Strait of Hormuz, and control by Ishmael (Iran), is a *drash* that connects an ancient text to modern geopolitics. This is a traditional Midrashic approach to finding contemporary relevance in prophecy, but it is not a literal, universally accepted geographical identification.
*   **Connecting "Persian King mocks Arab King" to current events:** The *Yalkut Shimoni* Midrash describes kings mocking each other. Rabbi Shapira's application of this to Iran's current actions against Arab neighbors is an interpretive lens through which to view contemporary events, seeing them as a fulfillment of the Midrash. While compelling, it is an interpretation of current events through a prophetic framework, not a literal, one-to-one prediction of specific modern nations.
*   **The timing of events in Nisan:** While Nisan is traditionally associated with redemption, the assertion that the "patriarchs are trembling in Nisan" and that the dialogue between the patriarchs and Messiah ben Ephraim occurs "in Nisan" are Midrashic statements. Rabbi Shapira's conclusion that "this is now" and that the Messiah's "release" will happen during a war with Persia *in Nisan* is a strong interpretive leap, suggesting a very specific and imminent fulfillment based on these Midrashic connections.
*   **The nature of Messiah ben Ephraim:** The concept of Messiah ben Ephraim (or Ben Joseph) is a traditional Jewish idea, but its specific details and relationship to Messiah ben David are subject to various interpretations within Jewish thought. Rabbi Shapira's identification of this suffering Messiah with Yeshua is a core tenet of Messianic Judaism, which is an interpretive theological position within the broader Jewish-Christian dialogue.

In summary, while Rabbi Shapira's message is powerful and spiritually significant, readers should approach it with an understanding that it represents a particular interpretive tradition that seeks to find contemporary meaning in ancient texts. It is a call to spiritual vigilance and prayer based on a specific *drash* of prophetic literature, rather than a literal, predictive forecast in the *peshat* sense.
